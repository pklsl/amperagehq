The garage is the workshop, EV charging station, and utility hub of the modern home — and its electrical requirements have evolved significantly to support these uses. From GFCI-protected receptacles to 240V EV charger circuits, this guide covers what the NEC requires for garages and what you should consider for a modern, functional garage electrical system.

## GFCI Requirements in Garages

### NEC 210.8(A)(2)

All 125-volt, 15-amp and 20-amp receptacles in a garage or accessory building attached to a garage must be GFCI protected. This applies to:

- All wall receptacles
- Receptacles at workbenches
- Receptacles in detached garages and outbuildings associated with a one- or two-family dwelling

**Exceptions:**
- Receptacles that are not readily accessible (such as a receptacle mounted high on a wall exclusively for a garage door opener) are exempt from GFCI protection under the 2023 NEC
- Receptacles for dedicated appliances or equipment (some interpretations allow exceptions for equipment requiring uninterrupted power like freezers — check with your AHJ)

GFCI protection in garages is critical because garages typically have concrete floors, vehicles dripping water and fluids, and power tools operated in conditions that increase shock risk.

## Garage Receptacle Requirements

### Spacing and Quantity

The NEC doesn't specify a minimum number of receptacle outlets in a garage (unlike habitable rooms). However:

- Any garage space **must have at least one receptacle** per NEC 210.52(G)
- The receptacle must be located where it's readily accessible from the vehicle parking area
- For garages with multiple vehicle spaces, at least one receptacle per parking space is required

In practice, a well-planned garage includes receptacles every 6–8 feet along workbench walls, and at least one on each wall where tools or equipment will be used.

### Receptacle Heights

NEC doesn't specify receptacle mounting heights for garages, but practical installation heights are:

- **Standard wall outlets:** 18–24 inches from floor (avoids water splash from flooding)
- **Workbench receptacles:** 44–48 inches (countertop height), or mounted to the underside of wall cabinets
- **Garage door opener:** 7–8 feet (non-accessible, GFCI exempt)

## Garage Lighting

### Required Lighting

The garage must have adequate lighting. Specific minimum illumination levels are set by building codes and local ordinances rather than NEC, but typical requirements:

- At least one switched lighting outlet (not just a pull-chain fixture)
- A switch at the entrance from the house provides convenience
- Separate lighting circuits from the receptacle circuit

### LED Shop Lights and Fluorescent Fixtures

Modern garage lighting uses LED shop lights (commonly 4-foot or 8-foot linear LED fixtures) that provide 4,000–8,000 lumens per fixture. For a 2-car garage:

- 4–6 fixtures of 4,000–5,000 lumens each provides good general illumination
- Position fixtures to minimize shadows under vehicle hood lines

### Lighting Circuit

Garage lighting is typically on a 15A or 20A circuit, separate from the receptacle circuit. Combining lighting and receptacles on a single circuit means a tripped breaker leaves you in the dark.

## Garage Subpanel

For garages with substantial electrical loads (workshop equipment, EV charger, air compressor), a subpanel is the most practical solution. A subpanel in the garage:

- Provides local overcurrent protection for garage circuits
- Allows easy addition of new circuits without running back to the main panel
- Supports 240V loads (table saw, welder, EV charger) through the feeder

**Sizing a garage subpanel:**

Consider your anticipated loads:
| Load | Circuit Size |
|---|---|
| General receptacles | 20A, 120V |
| Lighting | 15–20A, 120V |
| Air compressor (1–3 HP) | 20A, 240V |
| Table saw or band saw | 20A, 240V |
| Welder | 50A, 240V |
| EV charger (Level 2) | 50A, 240V |

A 60A or 100A subpanel feeder handles most workshop configurations. If you have large welders or multiple 240V loads, size to 100A or higher.

**Subpanel feeder wiring:**

| Subpanel Size | Minimum Wire Size (Copper) | Conduit Size |
|---|---|---|
| 60A | 6 AWG | 1" |
| 100A | 4 AWG | 1¼" |
| 125A | 2 AWG | 1¼" |

For detached garages, a 4-wire feeder (two hots, neutral, ground) is required for new work.

## EV Charger Wiring

### Level 1 vs Level 2 Charging

**Level 1 (120V):** Plugging the car into a standard 120V outlet. Adds 4–5 miles of range per hour. No special wiring needed.

**Level 2 (240V):** A dedicated 240V circuit with an EVSE (Electric Vehicle Service Equipment, commonly called a home charger). Adds 20–30 miles of range per hour, or fully charges most EVs overnight.

### Level 2 EVSE Installation Requirements

**Dedicated circuit:** A Level 2 EVSE requires a dedicated 240V, 2-pole circuit.

**Circuit sizing:**

The common EVSE sizes and their circuit requirements:

| Charger Output | Circuit Amperage | Wire Size (Copper) | NEMA Receptacle |
|---|---|---|---|
| 32A (7.7kW) | 40A dedicated | 8 AWG | NEMA 14-50 or hardwired |
| 40A (9.6kW) | 50A dedicated | 6 AWG | NEMA 14-50 or hardwired |
| 48A (11.5kW) | 60A dedicated | 6 AWG | Hardwired or NEMA 14-50 (with 60A breaker) |

**Standard recommendation:** A 50A dedicated circuit with a NEMA 14-50 receptacle accommodates most Level 2 chargers and provides flexibility to change chargers in the future.

**GFCI for EVSE:** Listed EVSEs include built-in GFCI protection. No separate GFCI breaker is required at the panel for the EVSE circuit.

### EVSE Location

Mount the EVSE at a location accessible from the driver's side door with the cord reaching the charge port without strain. Standard mounting height is 4–5 feet. Allow sufficient cord length for both parking positions in a 2-car garage.

### Future-Proofing for EVs

If you're doing electrical work in the garage and don't have an EV yet, consider running an empty conduit (1" EMT) from the panel to the EV charging location and pulling a 6 AWG feeder. The marginal cost at time of construction is minimal compared to retrofitting later.

## Workshop Electrical Planning

### Air Compressor

Most residential and light commercial air compressors (1.5–3 HP) run on a 20A, 240V dedicated circuit. Larger compressors (5+ HP) require 30A or 40A circuits.

- Wire: 12 AWG for 20A, 10 AWG for 30A
- Wiring must be in conduit or use appropriate cable type

### Table Saw and Other Woodworking Equipment

Cabinet saws and contractor saws typically run on 240V and draw 15–20A. Dedicate a 20A, 240V circuit to the table saw — it should not share with other large tools.

### Welders

Welders are among the highest-demand tools. A 240V welder:

- 140A stick welder: typically requires a 50A circuit
- MIG welder (200A): typically requires a 50A circuit
- TIG welder (200A AC/DC): similarly requires 50A

A 50A, 240V dedicated circuit covers most hobbyist welding equipment.

### Metal Shop / Grinder

A bench grinder typically runs on 120V and draws 8–15A. A 20A, 120V circuit at the bench covers grinders along with other hand tool charging.

## Detached Garage Electrical

A detached garage requires a separate electrical service or a feeder from the main panel. Key differences:

**4-wire feeder required:** For new installations to a detached structure, NEC requires a 4-wire feeder (two hots, one neutral, one ground). The neutral and ground are kept separate in the detached garage panel.

**Disconnecting means:** The garage must have a means to disconnect all power at the structure. This is typically the main breaker in the subpanel.

**Burial depth for underground feed:**
| Wiring Method | Burial Depth |
|---|---|
| Direct burial cable (UF-B) | 24 inches |
| PVC conduit | 18 inches |
| RMC or IMC | 6 inches |
| GFCI-protected circuits (120V) | 12 inches with PVC |

**Overhead service:** An overhead feed (service drop) from the house to the garage requires a minimum clearance of 10 feet above finished grade under the conductors. Conductor height requirements vary with the specific location and traffic below.

## Garage Electrical Checklist

**Receptacles:**
- [ ] All receptacles in garage are GFCI protected
- [ ] Garage door opener receptacle is non-accessible (high mount)
- [ ] At least one receptacle in the parking area
- [ ] Workbench has receptacles at convenient working height

**Lighting:**
- [ ] Garage lighting is on a separate circuit from receptacles
- [ ] Lights are switched at the entry point
- [ ] Illumination is adequate for workspace (at least 50 foot-candles at work surface)

**240V Circuits:**
- [ ] EV charger circuit is dedicated and correctly sized
- [ ] Workshop 240V equipment each on dedicated circuits
- [ ] Wire sizing correct for all circuit ampacities

**Subpanel (if installed):**
- [ ] Subpanel sized for anticipated loads with room to grow
- [ ] Neutral and ground bars are separate (not bonded, unless this is the only distribution point)
- [ ] All circuits labeled accurately

A well-planned garage electrical system adds significant value to a home and enables a wide range of workshop and EV charging capabilities. Investing in adequate capacity, proper GFCI protection, and organized circuitry when the walls are open is far less expensive than retrofitting later.
