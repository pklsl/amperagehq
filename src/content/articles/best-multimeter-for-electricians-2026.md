A multimeter is the single most important test instrument in an electrician's bag. The right meter can diagnose a tricky fault in minutes; the wrong one gives you false readings that waste hours — or gets you hurt. This guide compares the best multimeters across every price tier so you can make an informed choice.

## What to Look For in a Multimeter

Before diving into specific models, understand what separates a professional-grade meter from a cheap box-store unit.

### Safety Rating (CAT Rating)

The most critical spec on any meter is its CAT (category) rating:

- **CAT II** — household outlets and portable equipment
- **CAT III** — distribution wiring, motors, panels
- **CAT IV** — service entrance, outdoor conductors, utility connections

A meter rated CAT III 600V can handle panel work safely. CAT IV 600V is required for utility-side work. Never use a CAT II meter in a panel — transient voltages can blow right past the meter's protection.

### True RMS vs Average Responding

**True RMS** meters accurately measure non-sinusoidal waveforms — essential when working on circuits with VFDs, dimmers, motors, or any modern electronics. Average-responding meters underread by up to 40% on such loads. Every professional meter should be true RMS.

### Auto-ranging vs Manual Ranging

Auto-ranging selects the correct range automatically. Manual ranging is faster if you already know the expected value. Most modern meters offer both, with auto-range as default.

### Key Measurement Functions

- AC/DC voltage
- AC/DC current (built-in clamp or separate probe input)
- Resistance
- Continuity with audible beep
- Diode test
- Capacitance (useful for motor diagnosis)
- Frequency / duty cycle
- Temperature (via thermocouple)
- NCV (non-contact voltage detection)

### Display and Data Hold

A large, bright backlit display is essential in dim electrical rooms. Look for a minimum of 4,000 counts — professional meters typically offer 6,000 or more. Data hold, MIN/MAX recording, and relative mode are useful in the field.

## Best Multimeters by Price Tier

### Budget Tier (Under $60)

#### Klein Tools MM400

The MM400 is Klein's entry-level auto-ranging multimeter and delivers surprising value. It's CAT III 600V rated, true RMS, and includes a backlit display, audible continuity, and NCV detection via the port socket. Measurement categories cover the standard electrician's needs: AC/DC voltage, resistance, continuity, diode test, and frequency.

**Pros:** Affordable, rugged housing, CAT III rating
**Cons:** No clamp function, no temperature, 4,000 counts
**Best for:** Apprentices, homeowners doing basic troubleshooting

#### AstroAI AM33D

At around $30, the AstroAI AM33D gives you true RMS, auto-ranging, and CAT III 600V in a compact package. The display is smaller than Klein's but readable. It's a solid backup meter to keep in the truck.

---

### Mid-Range Tier ($60–$150)

#### Klein Tools MM700

A step up from the MM400, the MM700 adds a CAT IV 600V rating, built-in NCV, and a low battery indicator. The 6,000-count display provides better resolution at the lower end of ranges. It handles temperature with a Type-K thermocouple input and adds capacitance measurement — useful for HVAC work.

**Pros:** CAT IV rating, true RMS, capacitance, temperature
**Cons:** No built-in clamp function
**Best for:** Journeymen electricians, serious DIYers

#### Milwaukee 2216-20 (M12 Clamp Meter)

Milwaukee's cordless-ecosystem clamp meter is a favorite among electricians already invested in M12 tools. The 400A AC/DC clamp eliminates the need for separate clamps on most residential and light commercial work. It's CAT III 600V, true RMS, and includes a low Z voltage mode to prevent false readings from phantom voltage.

**Pros:** Built-in clamp, low Z mode, excellent build quality
**Cons:** Larger than a standard DMM, slightly pricier
**Best for:** Field electricians doing mixed troubleshooting and load measurement

#### Fluke 107

Fluke's entry into their own lineup, the 107 is a slim, pocket-sized auto-ranging meter rated CAT III 600V and CAT IV 300V. It uses a built-in clamp rated to 200A AC — no separate leads required for current. True RMS, backlit display, and a 10-year warranty make this a compelling value for Fluke quality at a mid-range price.

**Pros:** Compact, built-in clamp, Fluke reliability, long warranty
**Cons:** 200A clamp limit, smaller display
**Best for:** Electricians who want Fluke quality in a compact package

### Professional Tier ($150–$350)

#### Fluke 117

The Fluke 117 is the gold standard for residential and light commercial electrical work. Designed specifically for electricians, it features:

- **AutoVolt** — automatically switches between AC and DC
- **Low impedance mode (LoZ)** — eliminates false voltage readings from phantom voltage and inductive coupling
- **CAT III 600V / CAT IV 300V**
- **True RMS**
- **6,000-count display**
- Non-contact voltage detector built in

The LoZ mode alone is worth the price premium. Ghost voltages plague electricians using regular meters — the 117 cuts right through them. The ergonomics are excellent, with a one-handed operation design and clear mode labeling.

**Pros:** LoZ, AutoVolt, excellent ergonomics, industry standard reliability
**Cons:** No clamp function, no capacitance
**Best for:** Residential and commercial electricians as a primary troubleshooting meter

#### Fluke 116 HVAC

Similar to the 117 but optimized for HVAC technicians with built-in temperature measurement and microamp measurement for flame sensor testing. If you do both electrical and HVAC work, the 116 covers both disciplines.

#### Klein Tools CL800

Klein's professional clamp meter competes directly with Fluke's HVAC/electrical combo meters. The CL800 offers a 1,000A AC clamp, CAT IV 600V rating, true RMS, temperature, frequency, and low impedance mode — all in a package priced below Fluke's equivalent.

**Pros:** 1,000A clamp capacity, CAT IV 600V, competitive price
**Cons:** Display not as refined as Fluke
**Best for:** Electricians wanting a high-capacity clamp at a reasonable price

### Premium Tier ($350+)

#### Fluke 87V Industrial

The 87V is Fluke's flagship for industrial applications. Its key advantages:

- **20,000-count display** for exceptional resolution
- **True RMS with 100 kHz bandwidth** — accurate on high-frequency drives and switching supplies
- **Built-in thermometer** via Type-K thermocouple
- **MIN/MAX/Average recording** with 250ms response
- **CAT III 1000V / CAT IV 600V**
- **Audible and visual continuity**

The 87V excels on VFD-driven motors, inverters, and industrial control panels where waveform distortion makes lesser meters unreliable. It's an investment that pays off in industrial environments.

**Pros:** Exceptional accuracy, VFD-rated, heavy-duty build
**Cons:** Expensive, bulkier than residential meters
**Best for:** Industrial electricians, maintenance technicians

#### Fluke 376 FC

For electricians who primarily do current measurement alongside voltage work, the 376 FC combines a 1,000A AC/DC iFlex flexible clamp with a full-function DMM in one unit. The iFlex clamp wraps around conductors that won't fit a standard jaw. Wireless connectivity (Fluke Connect) lets you log readings to your phone.

**Pros:** iFlex flexible clamp, AC/DC current, wireless logging
**Cons:** Very expensive, overkill for residential work
**Best for:** Commercial/industrial electricians doing load surveys

## Fluke vs Klein: Which Brand Should You Choose?

**Fluke** has been the industry standard for decades. When an electrician says "grab my meter," they usually mean a Fluke. The 117 and 87V are the most common meters you'll see on job sites. Fluke's accuracy, safety testing, and support infrastructure are industry benchmarks.

**Klein Tools** offers comparable specs at 20–30% lower price points. For many electricians — especially those entering the trade — Klein delivers professional performance without the Fluke premium. The CL800 clamp meter in particular is a standout value.

**Milwaukee** excels for electricians already using M12 tools. Their meters integrate well with the ecosystem and prioritize durability and field usability.

**Bottom line:** Buy Fluke if budget allows and you want the industry-standard tool. Buy Klein if you want professional-grade at a lower price. Buy Milwaukee if you're in the M12 ecosystem.

## Maintaining Your Multimeter

A multimeter doesn't need much maintenance, but a few habits extend its life:

- **Replace the battery before it leaks** — a leaked battery can destroy the PCB
- **Inspect leads regularly** — cracked insulation is a safety hazard; replace at the first sign of damage
- **Store with fuses on-hand** — the current fuse blows if you accidentally measure voltage in current mode; keep spares
- **Keep the contacts clean** — a pencil eraser works on oxidized battery contacts
- **Calibrate annually if accuracy is critical** — most electricians calibrate their primary meters yearly
